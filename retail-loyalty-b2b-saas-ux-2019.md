# B2B Loyalty SaaS for Supermarkets — Full-Stack UX Across Research, IA, and Physical Touchpoints

A Brazilian loyalty and CRM SaaS for supermarket chains was undergoing a major platform redesign — from a legacy UI to a structured, UX-led product. The engagement spanned two years and covered nearly every UX workstream: information architecture from ontology design to screen-level wireframes, qualitative user research with supermarket operators, card sorting studies, onboarding flow design, and a physical kiosk sign-up experience for in-store shoppers. This was an embedded UX role on a product that was simultaneously being rebuilt and scaled.

## 1. Context

The client was a B2B SaaS company providing a loyalty program and customer relationship management platform to supermarket chains across Brazil. The platform covered dashboards, SMS campaigns, loyalty program management, customer profiling, campaign analytics, and in-store sign-up via totem kiosks. Clients were supermarket businesses; end users were their staff — marketing analysts, directors, and store managers — managing campaigns, customer data, and operational flows.

**The business challenge:** A legacy UI that had been built around early-stage product requirements, without structured UX process. As the client base grew and the platform expanded, the lack of IA foundations created compounding usability problems. The redesign initiative needed UX leadership across the entire product — not a single feature or screen.

**Complexity of the platform:** The product served multiple user types (marketing, directors, management, operations) with radically different access needs, different mental models for what "campaigns" meant, and different indicators of success. A supermarket director cared about TKTM (average transaction value) and faturamento (revenue); a marketing analyst cared about new cadastros (registrations) and campaign results by period.

**The research problem:** The platform had no formal user segmentation and no documented understanding of how different client types used (and didn't use) the platform. The Q4 2018 research was the first systematic attempt to address this gap.

## 3. Method

**Information architecture — ontology first.**
The platform had overlapping concepts that had accumulated without formal taxonomy. Before designing any screens, an ontology was produced in XMind: defining entities, their relationships, and the attributes that distinguished them. This fed into a comprehensive IA document (33MB) that became the redesign's structural foundation. Content inventory was produced in parallel.

**Q4 2018 user research — regional focus, in-person priority.**
Participants were supermarket chains in the state of Paraná, Brazil, prioritized for in-person visits. 10 clients were selected through a status review with the consulting team. 13 sessions total (8 in-person, 5 remote via Skype/Google Meet), with 17 respondents across marketing, directorship, management, purchasing, commercial, and finance roles. Dozens of supplementary phone and WhatsApp interactions to complete, update, and verify data not covered in the main sessions.

**Proto-persona synthesis.**
Results were organized by professional profile rather than individual, producing three proto-personas: Marketing Analyst (majority of respondents; 7 out of 17; platform's primary daily user; rated platform 7/10), Director (high-level strategic users; rated platform 8.6/10; prioritized revenue and TKTM), and Manager (operational; rated platform 9.5/10; primarily needed customer base administration and sector performance views). Where respondents performed multiple roles, data was allocated to the dominant profile.

**Card sorting — campaign and configuration navigation.**
Separate card sorting studies were conducted for two areas of the platform: campaign navigation (where features related to SMS campaigns, encarte campaigns, and results should live) and configuration settings (how users expected to find platform configuration options). Results were synthesized into navigation proposals for each area.

**Dashboard wireframes — Balsamiq iteration, XD export.**
Dashboard wireframes were produced in Balsamiq for rapid iteration, then exported to Adobe XD for stakeholder presentation and developer handoff. The dashboard covered 6 sections: Home, Client analytics, Campaigns, Clube (loyalty), SMS results, and a Magic Price Point modal.

**Totem sign-up flow — physical touchpoint design.**
The in-store kiosk sign-up experience for shoppers (cadastramento) required a separate UX treatment from the web platform. The flow was designed for a touchscreen kiosk context: different input constraints, different user (shopper, not staff), and different session time expectations. A standardized registration spec was produced for the client's IT team.

**Onboarding strategy.**
A comprehensive onboarding strategy was designed for new supermarket clients joining the platform — covering the registration flow, first-run experience, and the transition from setup to active use. This addressed the gap between "client signs contract" and "client staff can use the platform independently."

## 4. Analysis and Insights

**Insight 1 — Different user types had entirely different success metrics.**
Marketing analysts tracked campaign results and customer registration growth. Directors tracked revenue and average transaction value. Managers tracked sector performance and customer administration tasks. A platform dashboard designed for one profile would be actively misleading for the others. The redesign needed profile-aware dashboard views, not a single unified dashboard.

**Insight 2 — "Campaigns" meant different things to different people.**
Card sorting for the campaigns section revealed that users from different supermarket types organized campaign-related features very differently. Some grouped by channel (SMS, print, email); others by phase (creation, in-flight, results); others by target audience (all customers, loyalty members, new registrations). The navigation design had to accommodate multiple valid mental models.

**Insight 3 — Platform self-sufficiency was the top unmet need.**
A recurring theme across all three proto-personas was the desire to use the platform independently — without relying on the consulting team for routine tasks. The consulting team was perceived as doing "inalcançável" (unreachable) work. The platform's complexity was preventing clients from translating consulting insights into platform actions. This is a classic feature-adoption gap: the platform was powerful but not learnable.

**Insight 4 — The physical kiosk UX was an afterthought in the original product.**
The totem sign-up flow for shoppers was separate from the web platform in context, audience, and constraints — but had been designed with web-platform conventions. Touchscreen input, standing-up session time (30–90 seconds), and low-tech shopper users required a distinct design approach.

**Insight 5 — Client categories did not exist — making research generalization impossible.**
The platform had no formal client categorization beyond an informal size estimate. The research findings were therefore exploratory, not representative. The most important meta-finding was that the platform needed a client taxonomy before any customer segmentation feature could be built meaningfully.

## 6. Recommendations and Next Steps

1. **Formalize client categories** — before any user segmentation feature is built, the platform needs internal client classification criteria. The research cannot be generalized without them.
2. **Profile-aware dashboard** — the three proto-personas have incompatible primary indicators. A single dashboard serves none of them well.
3. **Onboarding self-sufficiency KPI** — measure whether clients can complete core tasks independently within 30 days. If the consulting team is still required for routine platform use after onboarding, the onboarding has failed.
4. **Totem UX separate track** — the in-store kiosk experience should be treated as a separate product for UX purposes — different users, different constraints, different success metrics from the web platform.

---

## 7. Reflection

**What worked well:**
- Conducting the ontology and formal IA before designing any screens. The platform had accumulated layers of product decisions without a shared vocabulary. The ontology resolved naming conflicts that would otherwise have appeared repeatedly in wireframe reviews.
- In-person research sessions in supermarkets — particularly with Marketing Analysts — revealed that the most frequent users were also the most underserved by the platform's information density. The insight ("they want analysis, not raw data") came from watching people use the platform at their own workstations, not from asking what they wanted in a survey.
- Separating the totem UX from the web platform explicitly. Treating them as the same design problem would have produced a kiosk that required staff-level platform literacy from shoppers in 30-second sessions.

**Challenges and constraints:**
- No formal outcome data is available for this retrospective entry. The research and design work was delivered; whether the platform metrics improved as a result is unknown.
- The engagement predates the structured project logging approach used in later work. This case was reconstructed retroactively from artifacts rather than from a real-time log.
- The "no client categorization" finding (Insight 5) was a meta-finding that required the client's internal data team to act on — outside the scope of UX delivery.

**What I'd do differently:**
- Propose a longitudinal follow-up study 6 months after the dashboard redesign to measure self-sufficiency rates. The research identified the gap; confirming whether the design closed it would have been the natural second step.
