# Home Furnishing E-Commerce — Understanding the Decision Journey Through Field Research

A large Brazilian home furniture and décor e-commerce platform commissioned a standalone research study to understand how customers made purchase decisions — not just what they did on the platform, but how the full decision process worked: researching, evaluating, deciding, and buying a piece of furniture. The engagement was a focused qualitative study using interviews and contextual field research, delivered across a 2–3 month period.

---

## 1. Context

The client was one of Brazil's major home furnishing e-commerce platforms — a category defined by high-consideration purchases. Buying furniture is not like buying a book: the decision cycle is long, involves spatial reasoning, often includes multiple stakeholders (partners, roommates, family), and carries high consequence for errors (returns are expensive; delivery is complex; items must fit a physical space). The platform had behavioral data from clickstream analytics, but no qualitative understanding of why users made the decisions they did or what happened outside the platform during the decision process.

**The research question:** What is the full decision journey for a furniture purchase — from awareness to installation — and where does the platform support or fail that journey?

**Engagement type:** A standalone research contract commissioned to an external UX research team. Not a continuous design relationship.

**Scope:** Qualitative exploratory research — interviews, contextual observation, photography. Deliverable: a research readout presentation with findings and recommendations.

## 3. Method

**Qualitative interviews.**
Semi-structured interviews with customers who had recently purchased or were in the process of purchasing furniture through the platform. The interview guide explored the full decision timeline — from recognizing the need, to researching options, to making the final choice, to post-purchase experience.

**Field research — contextual and ethnographic.**
Sessions conducted in the participants' homes or at the point of decision. This allowed observation of the actual context in which furniture was evaluated: the physical space the item would occupy, the measurement constraints, the existing furniture it needed to fit with, and the household dynamics that shaped the decision. Field photography was used to document contextual details that interview notes alone could not capture.

**Ethnographic photography.**
Visual documentation of the research context — not product photography, but documentation of the decision environment: rooms, measurement tools, comparison spreadsheets, mood boards, physical showroom visits by participants during the research period. These images were used in analysis and in the readout presentation to give stakeholders a concrete sense of the user's world.

**Thematic synthesis.**
Interview transcripts and observation notes were analyzed for patterns across participants. The analysis focused on decision phases (awareness, research, evaluation, purchase, post-purchase), pain points at each phase, and the role of the platform vs. off-platform resources (showrooms, social media, family input, interior design advice).

**Research readout.**
Findings and recommendations were presented to the product team in a structured presentation (3_Apresentação/). The format was findings-first with supporting evidence (quotes, field photos), followed by actionable recommendations for the product roadmap.

## 4. Analysis and Insights

*Note: Detailed findings are documented in the original research presentation. The following represents the key patterns available for retrospective reconstruction.*

**Insight 1 — Furniture decisions are rarely made alone.**
A significant portion of participants involved a partner, family member, or housemate in the final decision — often asynchronously (sharing screenshots, links, or photos for later review together). The platform was designed for single-session individual decision-making; the actual decision process was distributed and multi-session.

**Insight 2 — Physical space is the primary constraint, and the platform doesn't help with it.**
Participants described measuring rooms, checking whether items would fit through doors, and trying to visualize scale from product photos. The disconnect between the platform's 2D product photography and the 3D physical reality of the purchase was the most consistent source of anxiety in the decision process.

**Insight 3 — The return policy is a conversion factor, not just a post-purchase concern.**
Multiple participants described checking the return policy before purchasing — not because they planned to return the item, but because the possibility of return reduced the anxiety of a high-consequence decision. Return policy clarity (and trust in its execution) functioned as a pre-purchase confidence signal.

**Insight 4 — Contextual field research revealed details that interviews alone would not.**
Visiting participants' homes showed the actual conditions in which decisions were made: items measured against a wall with a tape measure, product pages open next to Pinterest boards, partners negotiating over mobile phones in different rooms. The ethnographic approach captured the social and spatial reality of the decision, which direct interview questions about "how do you shop for furniture" would not have surfaced.

## 6. Recommendations and Next Steps

1. **Multi-user decision support**: Add a "share for review" feature that allows users to share a product or shortlist with a co-decision-maker — not just a URL, but a shareable comparison view that works for someone who hasn't been browsing the session.
2. **Scale and spatial tools**: Provide an AR view or at minimum a room-scale reference tool that helps users visualize how a product fits in their actual space.
3. **Return policy as a conversion element**: Surface the return policy earlier in the purchase flow — on the product page, not just at checkout. Treat it as a trust signal, not a disclosure.
4. **Multi-session journey support**: Design for the reality that the decision is often made across multiple sessions, devices, and days — with wishlists, comparison tools, and saved sessions that make resuming the decision easy.

---

## 7. Reflection

**What worked well:**
- Field research (visiting participants in their homes) was the right method for this question. The contextual observations — rooms, tape measures, comparison spreadsheets, mood boards — were not discoverable through remote or lab-based interviews. The methodology matched the question.
- Ethnographic photography gave the product team a concrete visual reference for user context that abstract personas or journey maps could not provide. Images of a physical space being measured against a furniture product page communicate the problem more directly than any textual description.

**Challenges and constraints:**
- This is a retrospective entry; detailed session notes, synthesis artifacts, and outcome data are not fully reconstructable from available materials.
- The research was a standalone contract — there was no continuous relationship with the product team, so tracking whether findings were implemented is not possible from this position.

**What I'd do differently:**
- Structure a follow-up quantitative phase (survey or behavioral analysis) immediately after the qualitative findings were delivered — to test which of the qualitative insights applied at scale. The ethnographic findings were rich but based on a small participant set; validation would have strengthened the recommendations.
