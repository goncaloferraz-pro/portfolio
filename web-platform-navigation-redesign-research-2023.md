**Domain:** Web Hosting / SaaS

An enterprise web services platform was redesigning its global navigation — the top and footer menus across every page of its marketing site. The goal was to shift from a sprawling, inconsistently labeled menu toward a more conversion-focused structure that made it easier for visitors to find what they needed and take action.

My job was to answer five specific research questions before any design decisions were made.

## What I did

Over two months (April–June 2023), I led a multi-method research project combining four distinct activities: desk research, user interviews, card-sort, and usability testing.

**Desk research** established the baseline: I reviewed existing documentation (internal slide decks, listening posts, homepage heatmaps), analyzed the sitemap and content inventory, and benchmarked navigation patterns across competitor and reference sites — examining linear menus, mega menus, and Fitts' Law implications for click target sizing, especially on mobile.

**User interviews** explored how visitors mentally categorized the platform's products and services — what terms they used, what they expected to find where, and where terminology broke down.

**Card-sort** tested which groupings made the most sense to users. Based on user language and mental models, four top-level navigation categories emerged as the clearest structure:

- **Business** (for visitors looking to establish an online presence)
- **eCommerce** (for visitors building online stores)
- **Products** (for specific feature exploration and site updates)
- **Pricing** (consistently sought after a brief exploration of the site)

**Usability testing** validated the proposed navigation structure and surfaced interaction-level issues, including scroll position behavior when navigating between sections.

## What we found

The research answered all five research questions.

**Naming:** The platform's navigation relied on title-style sentences that omitted key terms — making it structurally unclear what each section contained. Users with lower familiarity with web technology struggled to locate what they were looking for. SEO was confirmed as an additional constraint requiring coordination with the marketing team.

**Groupings:** Two visitor profiles emerged: those exploring broadly, and those searching for known terms. Both groups responded to the four-category structure — Business, eCommerce, Products, Pricing — because it matched how they described their own needs. Pricing was consistently the item users navigated to after an initial page scan, reinforcing its prominence in the primary nav.

**Overview pages:** A single top-level "Overview" page per category was insufficient. Users expected overview pages to function as high-level landing pages — not as intermediate navigational steps. Product details should live on individual product pages with a clear CTA (e.g., "Contact Us" or "Add to Cart"), preventing deeper nav nesting that caused confusion.

**Menu type:** Given the volume of items in each category, a mega menu was recommended over a linear dropdown. Fitts' Law guided the recommendation: increasing click target size per item, particularly for mobile/touch screens, and using visual contrast to signal interactive areas. The research also found that pages should reset to the top of the scroll position when navigating via the main menu — a behavior users expected but the current implementation did not provide.

**Conversion focus:** Showing pricing tables at the end of Business and eCommerce category pages was well-received. Once users encountered interesting content, they expected to be able to learn more by clicking on it — suggesting that feature-level links from category pages were warranted.

## What changed

The research produced a set of specific, actionable recommendations for the product and design teams:

- Rewrite navigation labels to include key descriptive terms users recognize
- Restructure the primary nav into four validated groupings: Business, eCommerce, Products, Pricing
- Implement a mega menu to accommodate item volume and improve target sizing
- Limit support content to the Help Center channel only (removing the broader Resources list from the nav)
- Relocate Blog, Case Studies, and Whois Lookup to the footer, where they can be indexed by search engines without competing for nav real estate
- Reset scroll position to top when navigating between pages via main menu
- Surface pricing tables at the end of category pages

The study preceded any design or engineering investment — ensuring that navigation architecture decisions were grounded in observed user behavior rather than internal assumptions about terminology and structure.
