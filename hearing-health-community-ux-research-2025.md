# Cochlear Implant Community Platform — UX Improvement Proposal (Research Engagement, Not Approved)

> **Important context:** This engagement was a research and improvement proposal. The full plan was submitted to the client in October 2025 and was not approved at that time. A revised version was submitted in November 2025. The 28-item UX backlog was delivered. The broader improvement program (sprints 2–7: gap analysis, RBAC validation, prototyping, usability testing, PRD) did not receive client approval and was not executed. This case documents the proposal design and the one deliverable that was completed — not a delivered engagement.

A global cochlear implant community platform had been under development for over a year and was approaching soft launch. The platform served seven user profiles across 45 countries and had accumulated user feedback pointing to significant UX gaps. This engagement was a proposal for a systematic UX improvement program: a 7-sprint plan covering gap analysis for all profiles, RBAC validation, heuristic evaluation, Figma prototyping, usability testing, and a Product Requirements Document. The client approved the UX backlog delivery but did not approve the broader improvement program at the time of submission.

---

## 1. Context

The platform is an international social community connecting cochlear implant recipients, caregivers, peer mentors, and clinical professionals across 45 countries. The platform's seven user profiles have distinct access permissions, distinct journey patterns, and distinct unmet needs. By October 2025, the platform had accumulated feedback from operations (platform manager), user sessions, and a triangulation research concept — but no systematic UX review had been conducted.

**The problem:** Scattered feedback with no synthesis, no prioritization, and no plan. Individual bugs were being tracked, but the structural UX gaps across profiles — especially for Candidates (pre-implant users, most limited access) and Caregivers — had not been analyzed.

**The proposal:** A 7-sprint UX improvement program starting November 3, 2025, delivering by December 19, 2025. Scope: full gap analysis by profile, RBAC parity check, heuristic evaluation, Matomo analytics integration, prototype development, usability testing, and a final PRD.

**Why it was not approved:** The client decided not to proceed with the full program at that time. The 28-item prioritized UX backlog — the first deliverable — was completed and delivered.

## 2. My Role (analyzed from project log)

**Primary: Senior UX Researcher**
Designed a comprehensive UX improvement program covering gap analysis, RBAC validation, prototyping, and usability testing for a 7-profile platform; delivered the UX backlog despite the broader engagement being declined.

**Secondary roles:**
- **Research Ops**: Designed the sprint structure, deliverable tracking, and stakeholder review cadence for the proposed improvement program; produced the survey blueprint and interview guide for the triangulation research.
- **UX Auditor / Consultant**: Planned the heuristic evaluation, RBAC parity check, and functional QA as part of the proposed sprint 3–4 scope.

## 3. Method

**User feedback synthesis → UX backlog.**
Raw user feedback was provided by the platform operations team. The feedback was synthesized, deduplicated, and structured into a prioritized backlog of 28 items. Each item was tagged by affected profile (Candidate, Recipient, Caregiver, Mentor, Area Manager, Super Admin), severity (Critical → Lower), and bug type (Functional / UX Defect). The result was directly usable in a sprint planning tool.

**7-sprint improvement plan.**
The proposed program was structured as one-week sprints with weekly stakeholder reviews by profile segment:
- Sprint 1: Kick-off — profile confirmation, access setup, alignment meeting
- Sprint 2: Feedback ingestion — import and tag all feedback, begin gap analysis for Candidates and Recipients
- Sprint 3: Gap analysis (Part 1) — all 7 profiles, RBAC parity check begins, heuristic evaluation, profile checklists
- Sprint 4: Gap analysis (Part 2) — complete heuristics and RBAC validation, Matomo integration, functional QA
- Sprint 5: Stakeholder alignment — Rules & Roles Verification Report, segmented reviews, begin high-priority prototypes
- Sprint 6: Prototyping — final Figma prototypes for all profiles, admin persona coverage, test scripts
- Sprint 7: Testing and PRD — usability tests, SUS/task data, analysis, final PRD draft
- Handoff buffer: PRD, prototypes, analytics package, verification reports, defect log, QA summary

**Triangulation research design.**
A structured triangulation research plan was designed for the platform's four primary user segments: recipients (estimated 200–250), candidates (150–200), parents/caregivers (12–15), and mentors (8–10). The plan included a 36-item survey blueprint (8 sections: consent, demographics, usage, benefits, pain points, feature exploration, future vision, closing), an interview guide, a triangulation matrix, an analysis framework, and an 8-week timeline. The research was designed but not executed.

---

## 4. Analysis and Insights

*Note: The broader gap analysis (sprints 2–7) was not executed. The following insights are based on the feedback synthesis that produced the backlog, and on the gap analysis design.*

**Insight 1 — The Candidate profile had the most critical unmet needs.**
Of the seven platform profiles, Candidates (pre-implant users seeking information and peer connection) had the most restricted access and the least designed experience. The feedback synthesis indicated that Candidates could register and send questions to groups — but had no designed path from "received an answer" to the next step in their journey. The onboarding gap for this profile was identified as the highest-priority area for gap analysis.

**Insight 2 — Notification overload was affecting Mentor engagement.**
Mentors — who receive questions from multiple Candidates — reported notification fatigue. The platform had no notification grouping, no mark-all-read, and no badge cap. For a role whose value depends on staying engaged, notification overload was a structural risk to the mentorship model.

**Insight 3 — RBAC parity between spec and implementation was unverified.**
The platform's roles and permissions were defined in an aspirational spec document — but whether the implemented platform matched that spec had never been formally checked. The RBAC parity check was designed to close this gap. (This check was later completed in the March 2026 engagement — see the separate case on the March 2026 UX leadership engagement.)

**Insight 4 — The triangulation research was designed, not executed.**
The research plan was ready — 36-item survey, interview guide, 4-segment persona framework, 8-week timeline. Its non-execution means the platform's quantitative understanding of how each profile actually uses (and fails to use) the platform remains unmeasured. This gap was explicitly acknowledged in the engagement documentation.

**Deliverables produced:**
- `backlog-from-user-feedback.md` / `.csv` — 28-item prioritized UX backlog (Critical → Lower); tagged by profile and bug type
- `plan-ux-improvements-updated.md` — 7-sprint improvement plan with sprint schedule, deliverable list, and success metrics

- Interview guide (designed, not executed)
- Triangulation research plan (designed, not executed)

**Outcomes:**

- The broader 7-sprint program was not approved by the client at the time of submission. No prototypes, usability tests, or PRD were produced under this engagement.
- The RBAC parity check and gap analysis that were planned in this proposal were later completed under a separate engagement in March 2026.

---

## 6. Recommendations and Next Steps

1. **Execute the triangulation research** — the 36-item survey and interview guide are ready. The research would provide the platform's first quantitative baseline for engagement by profile type. Without it, improvement decisions rest on qualitative feedback and operational observation only.
2. **Close the RBAC parity gap** — the aspirational spec vs. implemented behavior gap was identified in this proposal and later verified as real (in the March 2026 engagement). The backlog items that depend on correct RBAC behavior cannot be safely resolved without this verification.
3. **Treat Candidate onboarding as the highest-priority improvement area** — the most restricted user profile with the least designed journey is also the one who most needs guidance. The absence of a post-answer CTA for Candidates is the most consequential structural gap.
4. **Notification system design as a Mentor retention intervention** — the mentorship model depends on Mentors staying engaged. Notification overload is a measurable risk to that model and should be treated as a critical platform-health issue, not a quality-of-life improvement.

---

## 7. Reflection

**What worked well:**
- Producing a concrete deliverable (the 28-item backlog) even when the broader engagement was not approved. The backlog provided the client team with an immediately usable prioritization artifact regardless of whether the full program proceeded.
- Designing the triangulation research in full before the engagement was confirmed, so it would be ready to execute immediately if approved. The 36-item survey blueprint and analysis framework required significant methodological design work that should not be deferred to the execution phase.

**Challenges and constraints:**
- The engagement was not approved. The most significant design and research work — gap analysis, RBAC validation, prototyping, usability testing — was planned but not executed. This means the findings from this engagement are based on feedback synthesis only, not on direct user research or heuristic evaluation.
- The platform was approaching soft launch at the time of the proposal. The tension between "the platform needs UX improvements before launch" and "the client needs to launch" was the context in which the proposal was evaluated — and likely contributed to the decision to defer.

**What I'd do differently:**
- Scope the initial proposal more narrowly — focus on the two highest-priority profiles (Candidates and Mentors) and the two highest-severity backlog items, rather than a full 7-profile, 7-sprint program. A smaller, immediately deliverable proposal might have been easier to approve under launch pressure.
- Explicitly connect each sprint's deliverables to launch risk, not just UX quality. Framing the RBAC parity check as "launch risk reduction" rather than "UX improvement" would have positioned it differently in the client's decision calculus.
