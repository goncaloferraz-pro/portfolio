# B2B Workflow Platform — Defining Activation Before Users Gave Up

A workflow management platform had declining retention and no clear picture of where users were dropping off — website, signup, or product. I ran a two-phase, **100-interview** program over 6 months, pivoted the research strategy mid-project based on a growth framework benchmark, and produced the evidence base that redefined what "activated" should actually mean for this product.

| | |
|---|---|
| **100** interviews across 2 research phases | **259** findings from potential customer sessions |
| **64** potential customer interviews (US + Canada) | **36** current customer interviews (US, Canada, Brazil) |
| **7** competitors benchmarked across 3 categories | **3** user segments with distinct onboarding needs defined |

---

## 1. Context

A workflow automation platform targeting HR and Finance teams in the US market was losing users before they reached their first meaningful moment with the product. Retention numbers were trending down even as the team worked on fixes — and nobody could agree on whether the problem was on the marketing site, in the signup flow, or inside the product itself.

The platform competed against category specialists (HR workflow tools, Finance automation tools) and horizontal alternatives (project management and no-code platforms). Enterprise buyers from larger companies consistently asked for demos instead of signing up. Smaller teams signed up and then disappeared. Both patterns were unknown territory.

The brief: find out where the funnel was breaking and define what "activated in 14 days since signup" should actually mean for this product.

---

## 2. My Role

**Primary: Principal UX Researcher**
I designed the full two-phase research program, led all 100 interviews, and continuously adapted research strategy based on emerging patterns — including a mid-project scope expansion triggered by an external growth framework benchmark.

**Secondary roles:**
- **UX Strategist**: Framed the activation problem using the Pirate AARRR framework; translated findings into two distinct onboarding strategies (PLG self-service vs. sales-assisted enterprise) with specific product implications.
- **Research Ops**: Maintained a weekly interview cadence across 12 weeks, adjusted screener criteria as segments emerged, managed participant sourcing across US, Canada, and Brazil cohorts.

---

## 3. Method

**Phase 1 — Potential customers (64 interviews, weeks 41–46)**
Participants: US and Canada, HR and Finance verticals, company sizes 51–200, 201–1000, 1001–5000, and 5000+. Sessions included side-by-side comparisons with 7 competitors across HR, Finance, and horizontal tool categories. Research strategy updated weekly. Goal: understand what was blocking consideration, onboarding, and activation.

**Mid-project pivot — OpenView benchmark**
A benchmark session with an external growth advisory firm (OpenView Partners) surfaced that activation best practices require 3 components: a time-based window (7–28 days), persona-specific onboarding paths, and High-Value Actions tied to measurable retention. None of these were formally defined for the platform. This reframed Phase 2: instead of studying why users left, we studied what retained users did differently.

**Phase 2 — Current customers and recent churners (36 interviews, weeks 48–52)**
Concentrated in Q4. Focus on AHA moments, WOW moments, top features, and data input behaviors. Goal: identify the specific product actions that predicted long-term retention.

**Analysis:** All sessions thematically coded. 259 findings from potential customer sessions categorized by journey step (Consideration: 80, Onboarding: 104, Activation: 75) and by problem theme. Company-size segmentation applied across all findings.

---

## 4. Analysis and Insights

**Finding 1 — The website was the biggest barrier (79 of 259 findings).**
Website problems outnumbered in-product problems by a wide margin. 64 out of the 100 potential customers were from larger companies — and those participants drove most of the website and signup friction. The value proposition was unclear; the connection between Pipefy and each visitor's specific use case was invisible.

**Finding 2 — Three user segments with fundamentally incompatible needs.**
Small companies (51–200) don't arrive with established processes — they build them inside the product. They onboard and activate more than any other segment. Medium companies (201–1000) have semi-mature workflows they need to transfer; their friction is about migration, not discovery. Large companies (1000+) have rigid processes, don't self-sign-up, and need to see the product working before committing to an internal demo request. One onboarding model cannot serve all three.

**Finding 3 — High-Value Features were invisible without a demo.**
Users who hadn't received a demo consistently failed to discover Automation, Process Customization, and Templates — the exact features that retained users valued most. Automation was the #1 AHA moment (15 mentions) and the #1 top feature for retained customers (14 mentions). Flexibility/Customization was the #1 WOW moment (12 mentions). Self-service discovery was not a reliable path to these features.

**Finding 4 — The walkthrough was the second-biggest onboarding problem.**
Walkthrough ineffectiveness scored 29 instances in the Onboarding findings — second only to Signup Barrier (36). Users couldn't skip the tour, encountered blocked interactions in the start form, and didn't see how the walkthrough connected to their actual use case.

**Finding 5 — A 1–2 week offline planning phase preceded all successful activations.**
Retained customers consistently described needing to sketch or plan their process on a whiteboard or in another tool before they could act inside the product. This phase was invisible to product analytics but determined whether users ever returned. The product was trying to activate users who weren't cognitively ready yet.

**Finding 6 — Public Form was the highest-value retention action.**
Among current customers, 35 used Public Form as their primary data input method — more than Start Form (10), Manual Input (10), and Portals (7) combined. Public Form was also the #2 top feature (12 mentions) and directly correlated with Portals as the greatest High-Value Action for long-term retention.

---

## 5. Deliverables and Outcomes

- Full research report (68-slide presentation) with findings segmented by company size, journey step, and problem theme
- 3 user segment profiles (Small / Medium / Large) with distinct onboarding implications and recommended paths
- High-Value Actions framework identifying Automations, Public Form, and Email Templates as the primary retention drivers
- Competitive onboarding analysis across 7 tools (Airtable, Monday.com, Notion, Kissflow, Nintex, Process Street, Quick Base)
- Activation definition: 14-day time window, 3 persona-specific paths, top High-Value Actions to instrument

**Strategic outcome:** The research produced a clear recommendation: replace the single-walkthrough model with two distinct onboarding paths — a self-service PLG path for small companies, and a demo/sales-assisted model for enterprise.

In 2021, the platform launched a redesigned product that simplified onboarding, surfaced high-value features, and dropped the blocking walkthrough. The platform **doubled its ARR** that year and raised a **$75M Series C**, with investors citing the platform's ability to scale from a single team to an entire enterprise as a primary rationale — the exact segmentation this research defined.

---

## 6. Recommendations and Next Steps

Top recommendations delivered:

1. **Redesign the marketing site** to clarify value proposition and use-case fit — most impactful for larger companies who evaluate from the outside before trialing
2. **Build two distinct onboarding paths**: lightweight self-service for small teams; demo-request and sales-assisted flow for enterprise
3. **Surface High-Value Actions earlier** — Automations, Public Form, and Email Templates are what make users stay; the walkthrough doesn't get users there
4. **Replace the blocking walkthrough** with contextual nudges tied to actual user tasks and process-building moments
5. **Instrument AHA moment tracking**: pipe with public form activation, automation rule creation, and template use as leading retention indicators

Follow-up research flagged:
- Data-backed competitive analysis to supplement qualitative benchmarking
- Profile × problems cross-reference to validate company-size segmentation with behavioral data
- Ongoing education effectiveness analysis (demos vs. videos vs. in-product)

---

## 7. Reflection

**What worked:** The mid-project pivot was the most consequential decision of the engagement. Starting with a retention brief, the initial scope covered only Acquisition + Activation. The OpenView benchmark session revealed that defining activation required understanding the full retention journey — and that the platform hadn't defined its High-Value Actions yet. Expanding scope mid-project, while disruptive to the interview schedule, produced the second-phase findings that made the most strategic difference.

The multidisciplinary team approach to analysis was also effective: having product, design, and growth stakeholders analyze interview transcripts alongside UX reduced confirmation bias and produced richer interpretations of the same data.

**Challenges:** The absence of reliable in-product behavioral analytics was a persistent constraint. The research identified *which types* of actions predicted retention, but the team lacked event tracking to measure them at scale. The 1–2 week offline planning phase was discovered only through interviews — it was entirely invisible to product data.

**What I'd do differently:** Establish a behavioral data audit in parallel with Phase 1 to identify which user actions were and weren't being tracked. This would have allowed the High-Value Actions framework to be quantitatively validated immediately, rather than requiring follow-up instrumentation work after the research concluded.
