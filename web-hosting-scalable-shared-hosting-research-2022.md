# Scalable Shared Hosting UX Research — Web Services Platform

**Domain:** Web Hosting / SaaS
**Type:** Mixed-Methods UX Research (Prior Research Synthesis, Customer Interviews, Mockup Evaluation, Prototype Testing)
**Year:** 2022
**Role:** Senior UX Researcher

An enterprise web services platform was losing high-traffic small and medium business (SMB) customers at a critical point in their growth. When these customers outgrew the shared hosting tier, the platform's upgrade path pushed them to VPS or dedicated plans — which many found too complex and too expensive. Rather than upgrading, many migrated to competitors offering plans at 5–10× the monthly cost, choosing the pain of migration over the complexity of the upgrade.

The question was whether a more capable shared hosting tier — something between entry-level shared and full VPS — could retain these customers. But first, the team needed to understand what these customers actually needed, what they found confusing, and how the current experience failed them at the moment they most needed clarity.

## What I did

Working from late August to late September 2022, I led a **four-phase mixed-methods study** designed to move from existing knowledge to validated insights.

**Phase 1 — Prior Research Synthesis.** I analyzed a body of existing customer research across the platform's hosting tiers — prior surveys and lean interviews from the preceding two years — to identify what was already known about shared, VPS, and dedicated hosting customers before conducting new research.

**Phase 2 — Customer Interviews.** I recruited and interviewed SMB customers who had needed to upgrade from shared hosting. The research focused on three questions: Were their needs being met by the new plan? What difficulties were they experiencing? And would they have preferred a more powerful shared hosting plan over a full VPS or dedicated solution?

**Phase 3 — Mockup Evaluation.** Mockups of a proposed in-product alert and resolution flow were evaluated for clarity and tone. The core scenario: a customer receives an alert that their site is experiencing performance issues due to high traffic, and needs to understand what's happening and decide what to do next.

**Phase 4 — Prototype Testing.** The refined prototype was tested with a screened sample of **251 respondents**, with **86 participants (34%)** accepting research invitations. The sample was **100% SMB** and **70% non-web professionals** — customers managing their own sites without technical support staff.

## What we found

The prior research confirmed a persistent pattern: customers in the shared hosting tier primarily wanted to host more websites, handle more traffic, and get more storage. When performance degraded, the reasons that drove upgrades to VPS were Speed, Fully managed support, and High Availability — but the top reasons for *cancelling* VPS were Lack of guidance and the product not meeting their needs. The upgrade path created new problems without resolving the original ones.

The usability study surfaced issues across three levels of the customer experience:

**Interaction level:** The alert email's call-to-action was too passive — participants expected a more direct, action-oriented label. Resolution options weren't presented in a prioritized order; customers wanted to see no-cost resolution options (self-service, contacting support) before being offered a paid upgrade. Participants also couldn't identify *where* the problem was originating — which website, plugin, or component — making it impossible to act without contacting support.

**Journey level:** The upgrade plans weren't available for immediate purchase at the moment customers were most motivated to act. The plan comparison page needed clearer pricing, descriptions, and structure. The knowledge base landing page was too generic to help customers troubleshoot in the moment they needed it.

**Relationship level:** Customers who encountered performance issues immediately began looking for alternative hosting providers. The research showed that alerting customers about a performance problem — without providing a clear, self-service path to resolution — accelerated rather than prevented churn. Customers needed to feel that the platform was a trusted partner, not a vendor pushing them toward a more expensive product.

## What changed

The research produced structured, actionable recommendations across all three levels, organized so the product team could prioritize by impact and implementation effort.

Key directions: present no-cost resolution options before upgrade offers; identify and surface the specific CPU-consuming component in the alert; keep upgrade plans unavailable rather than showing them as coming soon (which frustrated customers further); and create a purpose-built troubleshooting page rather than directing customers to a general knowledge base.

The study gave the product team a validated view of the upgrade journey's failure modes — and evidence that a higher-performance shared tier, positioned correctly, could address the retention problem without forcing customers into VPS complexity they neither wanted nor needed.
