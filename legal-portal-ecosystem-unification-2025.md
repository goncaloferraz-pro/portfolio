# State Bar Association — Unifying a Fragmented Digital Ecosystem

A state bar association serving **50,000+ legal professionals** operated a fragmented network of portals — each with its own login, navigation logic, and visual language. I led a research and IA strategy engagement that diagnosed the fragmentation, mapped **140+ services**, and designed a three-tier architecture that reframed the project from a visual redesign into an organizational change argument.

| | |
|---|---|
| **140+** services and forms mapped across 4 portals | **4** isolated sub-environments with separate logins |
| **12** critical heuristic violations documented | **3** access tiers designed for fundamentally different user profiles |
| **6.2 / 10** baseline usability score | **40%** projected reduction in Level 1 support inquiries |

---

## 1. Context

The bar association operated a "digital archipelago": a main portal plus three separate sub-environments (continuing legal education, member benefits, and pension/financial services), each with its own identity, navigation logic, and login. Attorneys managing routine professional obligations — renewing licenses, tracking continuing education, accessing benefits — were forced to context-switch across disconnected systems daily.

The organization had engaged a digital contractor to unify the experience. But the project brief was incomplete: it framed the problem as a visual redesign. Before any design work began, the UX engagement needed to answer a more fundamental question: what should unification actually mean for users with radically different needs — public visitors, law graduates, and practicing attorneys?

---

## 2. My Role

**Primary: Principal UX Strategist**
I created the three-tier hypersegmentation architecture — the strategic framework that reframed unification from "one portal for everyone" into "distinct environments for distinct user profiles," eliminating the conditional logic that made a single interface unworkable.

**Secondary roles:**
- **UX Researcher**: Led heuristic evaluation, service mapping interviews, and card-sorting with stakeholder groups to define service priority tiers.
- **UX Auditor / Consultant**: Produced the full diagnostic report series (executive summary, technical diagnosis, heuristic evaluation, methodology, and IA specification), with fragmentation evidence and severity ratings.
- **Information Architect**: Defined the full information architecture and navigation model for the proposed unified portal, including the attorney workspace task taxonomy.

---

## 3. Method

**Systems audit:** Mapped all digital properties under the association's umbrella — the main portal plus the three sub-environments. Produced a complete service inventory of **140+ services and forms**, including category, owner, audience, and usage frequency.

**Heuristic evaluation:** Evaluated the current ecosystem against Nielsen's 10 usability principles. Documented **12 critical violations**, producing an overall usability score of **6.2/10**. Navigation was structured around the organization's internal departments — not around what attorneys needed to accomplish.

**Card sorting and stakeholder workshops:** Facilitated structured sessions with internal stakeholders to classify all services by business impact and usage frequency. Weighted scoring produced a definitive service hierarchy and MVP roadmap. This step was critical: without it, every department believed their services were the priority. Key outputs: protocol consultation and treasury services elevated to maximum priority.

**Service mapping:** Mapped all 140+ services to three user audiences (Public, Citizens/Graduates, Practicing Attorneys) and to five usage frequency tiers, producing the classification matrix that drove IA decisions.

---

## 4. Analysis and Insights

**Finding 1 — The problem was fragmentation of purpose, not just fragmentation of systems.**
The homepage tried to serve everyone simultaneously: institutional news mixed with critical attorney tools, public transparency mixed with members-only services. No user group could efficiently complete their primary tasks. Each sub-environment had grown to serve internal organizational needs, not user needs.

**Finding 2 — Attorneys don't want to "visit the bar association website" — they want to resolve specific problems.**
The existing structure was organized around *who owns the service* (Treasurer's Office, ESA, CAA, OABPrev) rather than *what the user is trying to do* (My Finances, My Continuing Education, My Benefits). This is a jobs-to-be-done architecture failure at the organizational level.

**Finding 3 — Three user profiles with incompatible needs cannot share a single navigation.**
Public visitors needed institutional transparency and an attorney directory. Law graduates and exam candidates needed a one-time onboarding path to membership. Practicing attorneys needed a task-oriented workspace for daily professional obligations. Serving all three from the same IA required progressive disclosure so complex it degraded all three experiences.

**Finding 4 — Multiple separate logins were the primary daily friction for attorneys.**
The four sub-environments each had independent authentication. Attorneys managing continuing education, financial matters, and bar obligations had no single point of entry — and no shared session context between systems.

**Finding 5 — Regional offices were an invisible fragmentation layer.**
The 50+ regional offices operated independent websites with no consistency. They were outside the main unification scope but represented the same IA failure replicated at scale, and required a standardizable template solution.

## 5. Deliverables and Outcomes

**Deliverable series (published documents):**
- Executive summary: unification strategy, diagnosis, and three-tier architecture rationale
- Unification strategy document with transition roadmap
- Information architecture and navigation specification
- Technical subsection IA spec
- Technical diagnosis and fragmentation evidence
- Heuristic evaluation and full heuristic report
- Research and prioritization methodology
- Services and tasks inventory

**The three-tier architecture:**
- **Layer 1 — Public Portal**: Institutional content, attorney directory, transparency, SEO-optimized, no login required
- **Layer 2 — Transitional Users**: Bar exam candidates and non-practicing members with self-service onboarding flows
- **Layer 3 — Attorney Workspace**: Task-oriented dashboard integrating continuing education, benefits, pension, financial management, and electronic filing from one place, one login, one search — navigation restructured from department-centric (Treasurer's Office) to task-centric (My Finances)

**Projected outcomes:** SSO consolidation to eliminate 4 separate login systems; self-service improvements projected to reduce Level 1 support inquiries by **40%**; standard administrative tasks running at 10–20 days re-architected to 48-hour completion; standardized template for 50+ regional offices.

---

## 6. Recommendations and Next Steps

Top recommendations delivered:

1. **Implement SSO immediately** — unified authentication is the single highest-impact improvement for attorney satisfaction and is a prerequisite for any portal unification
2. **Restructure navigation from department-centric to task-centric** — attorneys should see "My Finances" and "My Continuing Education", not organizational unit names
3. **Redesign the homepage for public/transitional audiences only** — attorneys should bypass it entirely via direct login to their workspace
4. **Prioritize protocol consultation and treasury services** in the MVP attorney workspace — workshop data confirmed highest usage frequency and business impact
5. **Build a standardized regional office template** as a second phase — without it, the ecosystem will remain fragmented at the periphery

Follow-up research recommended:
- Post-implementation usability testing with attorneys across all three profile types
- Quantitative validation of service priority tiers through usage analytics post-launch

---

## 7. Reflection

**What worked:** The hypersegmentation framework was the most powerful concept in the engagement. Counterintuitively, proposing *more* environments rather than one unified portal resolved the core tension — it eliminated the progressive disclosure complexity that was degrading every user group. Presenting this reframe to stakeholders was a pivotal moment: it shifted the conversation from "how do we make the navigation work for everyone?" to "who are we actually designing for, right now, in this session?"

The card-sorting and stakeholder workshop were essential for navigating organizational politics. A fragmented portal is often the product of fragmented ownership — each department protecting their surface. The weighted scoring process gave every stakeholder a voice while producing a single agreed-upon priority list that no individual department could contest.

**Challenges:** The engagement was consulting-heavy rather than research-heavy. There was limited direct access to end users (attorneys) during the diagnostic phase. The heuristic evaluation and service mapping were expert-driven. Attorney interviews would have strengthened the finding on job-to-be-done navigation structure and surfaced the mobile usage pattern (court-appointed attorneys in particular) with more specificity.

**What I'd do differently:** Structure at least one user research round (interviews or diary study with practicing attorneys) into the early phase, before the IA proposal. The stakeholder workshops confirmed the service hierarchy, but attorney behavior data would have made the three-tier architecture case stronger in client presentations.
