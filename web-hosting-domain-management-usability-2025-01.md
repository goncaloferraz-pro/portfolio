# Domain Management Usability Study — Web Hosting / Enterprise SaaS

**Domain:** Web Hosting — Enterprise SaaS
**Type:** Moderated Remote Usability Testing
**Year:** 2025
**Role:** Senior UX Researcher

---

| | |
|---|---|
| **15+ participants** across 2 rounds | **7 critical usability issues** identified pre-launch |
| **5 core scenarios** tested per round | **2 rounds** with design iterations between |
| All **5 priority design changes** implemented | Core step-by-step workflow **validated as intuitive** |

---

An enterprise web services platform was **redesigning its domain management experience** — the flows users follow to purchase a new domain or connect one they already own elsewhere. The stakes were high: domain management is a primary acquisition and retention driver, and confusing terminology or hidden entry points translate directly to abandonment and support costs. 

My job was to find the friction before it reached production.

## What I did

Over two weeks (January 2025), I planned and led a **multi-round moderated remote usability study** using Figma prototypes tested through the [UserTesting platform](https://www.usertesting.com/). I developed the test plan, designed **5 task scenarios** covering the full domain management journey — from discovering the entry point to purchasing, connecting, and handling edge cases — and conducted sessions using a think-aloud protocol to capture mental models alongside task performance.

**Round 1** ran with **8 participants** — domain purchasers and website owners with varied technical backgrounds. After synthesizing findings and delivering a prioritized set of design recommendations, the product team iterated on the prototype. **Round 2** ran with **7 fresh participants** to validate the improvements and surface any issues that emerged from the fixes.

I also produced a condensed **video highlight reel** of key pain points for stakeholder presentations — allowing product and engineering leadership to see the usability failures rather than just read about them.

## What we found

The research identified **7 critical usability issues** in Round 1, with the most significant clustering around three root causes: 

- terminology mismatch, 
- low-visibility interaction elements, and 
- inadequate system feedback.

The most pervasive issue was a **mental model mismatch between "connect" and "purchase"**: users attempting to buy a domain consistently failed to recognize the intended entry point because the interface used the word "connect" — which they associated with linking something they already owned, not with a purchase flow. This confusion persisted across multiple participants and testing scenarios.

The secondary issue was a **misplaced and visually alarming "I own this domain" button**: styled in red (triggering a warning perception) and positioned far from the domain name it related to, it caused two participants to miss it entirely and three to feel uncertain about clicking it.

Round 2 confirmed that the core mental model mismatch had not been fully resolved by the first iteration. Users continued navigating through quick links, website buttons, and left-hand navigation searching for "purchase" or "buy" language, finding neither. Persistent issues also included unclear system feedback (the confirmation message read "domain added" regardless of whether a domain had been purchased or connected) and visual ambiguity between external and purchasable domain cards.

Positively, the underlying step-by-step workflow structure was consistently praised: once users found the correct entry point, the progression was clean, uncluttered, and easy to complete.

## What changed

Five priority design changes were implemented before development began:

- prominent "purchase" and "buy" language added throughout the flows, 
- visual redesign and repositioning of the "I own this domain" button, 
- placement of the Next Steps section above the fold on all viewports, 
- precise system feedback messages distinguishing "purchased" from "connected," and 
- pricing transparency improvements surfacing total costs earlier in the flow.

The two-round approach proved essential: Round 1 caught the critical structural issues; Round 2 validated which fixes held and revealed more nuanced problems that only emerged after the primary friction was removed. **Preventing 7 critical issues from reaching production** reduced the risk of post-launch support burden and stakeholder rework, with design changes confirmed and implemented before the development sprint began.
