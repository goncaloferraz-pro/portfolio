# Platform Migration Customer Feedback Analysis — Web Hosting / Enterprise SaaS

**Domain:** Web Hosting — Enterprise SaaS
**Type:** Mixed-Methods UX Research
**Year:** 2024
**Role:** Senior UX Researcher

---

|---|---|
| **999** customer responses analyzed | **2** legacy platforms migrated to one unified system |
| **8** critical improvement areas with specific actions | **~90,000** emails lost in a single migration incident |
| **4** cross-functional teams briefed | Q4 2024 + Q1 2025 product roadmap directly influenced |

---

When an enterprise web hosting company acquired two competitor platforms and migrated thousands of customers to its unified system, initial signals pointed to significant dissatisfaction — but the scope and root causes were unknown. 

My job was **to turn nearly 1,000 unstructured customer responses into a clear, prioritized action plan** across product, engineering, support, and leadership.

## What I did

Over four weeks (August–September 2024), I led a mixed-methods analysis of **999 customer responses** across three data streams: 

- Voice of Customer surveys (May–July 2024), 
- an in-product feedback survey (July 2024), and 
- qualitative interviews (July–August 2024).

The two customer cohorts were analyzed separately and then compared: **450 responses** from Platform A — a domain-focused registrar with a high proportion of long-tenured resellers and power users — and **549 responses** from Platform B — a website and email-focused platform serving small business owners with varied technical skill levels.

Methods included: 

- thematic coding of open-ended responses, 
- sentiment analysis across feedback categories, 
- issue frequency and severity mapping, and a 
- cross-platform comparative analysis to distinguish shared pain points from platform-specific failures.

## What we found

Both cohorts shared the same top three pain points despite coming from fundamentally different products: 

- **service quality degradation**, 
- **feature parity gaps**, and 
- **communication failures** throughout the migration.

For Platform A customers, the most critical incident was data integrity: a single migration event caused the loss of **~90,000 emails** for affected accounts, with no recovery path or compensation communicated. Long-tenured customers — many 10+ years — reported active intent to transfer their domains to competitors. The platform had silently removed features they relied on, with no equivalent alternatives provided.

Platform B customers hit a wall of missing functionality: web forwarding, email alias management, and nameserver editing had all been deactivated on the unified platform. SSL certificate reliability was broken. Customers received no advance notice of the merger and no documentation explaining what had changed or why.

A secondary analysis of the unified platform's website builder revealed deeply polarized satisfaction: **20% very satisfied** against **45% dissatisfied or very dissatisfied** — the gap driven primarily by technical failures and inaccessible support.

Across both cohorts, one theme was universal: **customers felt blindsided**. No migration guidance, no system-difference documentation, no proactive outreach.

## What changed

The research produced **8 prioritized improvement areas** — each with specific, actionable recommendations — covering: 

- service quality restoration, 
- feature parity recovery, 
- communication and change management, 
- data integrity protocols, 
- usability enhancements, 
- legacy service commitments, 
- customer retention programs, and 
- ongoing migration quality monitoring.

Findings were presented across **4 cross-functional teams**: 

- Product (feature parity roadmap), 
- Engineering (critical bug prioritization), 
- Customer Success (support strategy and staff training), and 
- Executive leadership (business impact and retention risk assessment). 

The research directly influenced the **Q4 2024 and Q1 2025 product roadmap** and created the foundation for an ongoing migration quality monitoring program.
