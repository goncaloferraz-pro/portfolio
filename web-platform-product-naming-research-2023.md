# Web Services Platform — Finding the Right Product Name Before Launch

A web services platform was launching a new WordPress plugin for small business owners and needed to choose from competing product names. I designed and ran a **four-stage mixed-methods process** — from criteria definition to validated scoring — with **110 participants** across two survey rounds, producing a clear winner and documented rationale for the final decision.

| | |
|---|---|
| **4 research stages** from knowledge review to validation | **110 total participants** across two survey rounds |
| **60 participants** in Proof of Concept Survey | **50 participants** in Validation Survey |
| **6 candidate names** evaluated across **5 criteria** | Winning name scored **5.6 / 6** on preferred name scale |

---

## 1. Context

A web services platform was preparing to market a new WordPress plugin designed to simplify website building for small business owners and creative professionals — novice or beginner WordPress users, primarily in creative fields or running their own businesses.

The marketing team had generated a list of potential product names, but needed a defensible basis for choosing one. Internal teams had strong preferences that didn't necessarily align. The research challenge: produce a recommendation that was grounded in what target users actually responded to — not what the team liked.

---

## 2. My Role

**Primary: Senior UX Researcher**
I designed the four-stage research process end to end: defined evaluation criteria, built and ran two surveys, facilitated the stakeholder workshop, and synthesized all stages into a ranked, scored recommendation.

**Secondary roles:**
- **Research Ops**: Defined the 5-criteria evaluation framework that structured all subsequent stages; built the scoring composite that allowed quantitative comparison across qualitative preferences.

---

## 3. Method

**Stage 1 — Knowledge Review**
Established the criteria that define a strong product name for this context: **memorability**, **ease of pronunciation**, **global appeal**, **understandability**, and **positive semantic associations**. These became the consistent evaluation dimensions for every subsequent stage — preventing the research from defaulting to simple preference ranking.

**Stage 2 — Proof of Concept Survey (60 participants, Maze)**
Recruited 60 WordPress users — predominantly novice/beginner, 65% in creative fields or company owners, matching the target demographic. Participants were asked to describe WordPress in three words (capturing user language before exposure to name candidates) and to vote on preferred terms from the initial name list.

Results: "Design" received the most votes (12), "Creator" came second (10). The survey revealed participants generally found WordPress easy to use but expressed mixed confidence with its terminology. This shaped which naming directions to develop.

**Stage 3 — Creative Workshop (internal stakeholders)**
Facilitated a workshop with marketing team members and project managers. Participants generated new name ideas, improved on existing candidates, and voted on preferences. The session produced a refined list of 6 names to take into final validation — incorporating both user-language signals from Stage 2 and internal creative directions.

**Stage 4 — Validation Survey (50 participants)**
Recruited 50 participants representative of the target audience. Each rated all 6 names across the 5 criteria established in Stage 1, producing composite scores for comparison. This stage was the quantitative resolution to the internal disagreement.

---

## 4. Analysis and Insights

**Finding 1 — One name outperformed all others across the criteria that matter most for adoption.**
Wonder Blocks led on preferred name, ease of recall, ease of pronunciation, and positive associations. Its composite score (5.6/6) was meaningfully above the second-place name (5.0/6) and well above the field.

**Finding 2 — The "WP" prefix pattern consistently underperformed.**
Four of the six candidate names used a "WP [noun]" construction. All four underperformed relative to the two names without the prefix. Participants found "WP" names more technical-sounding and harder to distinguish from each other. The two-word non-prefix construction ("Wonder Blocks," "WonderKit") was consistently better received by a non-technical audience.

**Finding 3 — "WP Content Kit" was last on the most important criteria.**
The lowest-scoring name scored 2.6/6 — weakest on preferred name, memorability, pronunciation, and understandability. It was the most generic of the six options and produced the least positive associations.

**Finding 4 — User language before exposure matters.**
The Stage 2 word-association exercise revealed that target users describe their WordPress experience with words like "Design," "Create," and "Build" — not with technical terms. Wonder Blocks' construction (simple, tactile, building-metaphor) aligned naturally with this language without requiring the "WP" technical marker.

---

## 5. Deliverables and Outcomes

- Ranked, scored recommendation report with composite scores for all 6 names across all 5 criteria
- Rationale documentation for each stage's contribution to the final recommendation
- Stakeholder workshop facilitation output (votes, generated options, refined shortlist)

**The four-stage structure provided a narrative the team could trust:** criteria were defined before any names were evaluated; user language was gathered before users were asked to judge; internal stakeholders were integrated; and quantitative evidence was used to resolve the disagreement that pure preference ranking would have left open.

The final naming decision rested with the marketing team, who received the recommendation and full scoring data to make and defend it.

---

## 6. Recommendations and Next Steps

Top findings delivered:

1. **Wonder Blocks** — clear winner; recommended for adoption; 5.6/6 composite across all criteria
2. **Avoid the "WP" prefix pattern** for products targeting non-technical users — the construction signals technical complexity to a beginner audience
3. **Test global linguistic concerns for "Wonder Blocks"** if the product will be marketed in non-English markets — "Wonder" has strong English connotations that may not translate directly

Follow-up research recommended:
- In-market name recognition study 6–12 months post-launch to track whether the name registered correctly with the target audience
- Localization assessment for priority international markets if the plugin is to be marketed outside the US/Canada

---

## 7. Reflection

**What worked:** The four-stage structure was the right defense against the most common failure mode in naming research: asking users to rank names without first establishing what criteria matter. By defining the evaluation dimensions before any exposure to candidates, the research produced a scoring framework the team trusted — not just a popularity contest.

The Proof of Concept Survey's word-association exercise was particularly useful. Capturing how users describe the existing product before showing them new names revealed the natural language the names would be competing against. Wonder Blocks fit that language; the WP-prefixed names didn't.

**Challenges:** The sample for both surveys was recruited through the research platform, which skews toward people comfortable with online surveys — not necessarily representative of the least-tech-savvy end of the target audience. True beginners are harder to recruit than intermediate users who self-identify as beginners.

**What I'd do differently:** Add a recall test to the Validation Survey: show participants all 6 names, then ask them to write back as many as they can remember after a 48-hour delay. Memorability is a criterion, but testing it in a single session (where all names are still in short-term memory) doesn't capture real-world recall. A delayed recall test would have produced more valid memorability scores.
