# Product Naming Research — Web Services Platform

**Domain:** Web Hosting / SaaS
**Type:** Mixed-Methods Product Naming Research
**Year:** 2023
**Role:** Senior UX Researcher

---

| | |
|---|---|
| **4 research stages** from knowledge review to validation | **110 total participants** across two survey rounds |
| **60 participants** in Proof of Concept Survey | **50 participants** in Validation Survey |
| **6 candidate names** evaluated against 5 criteria | Winning name scored **5.6 / 6** on preferred name scale |

---

A web services platform was preparing to launch a new WordPress plugin designed to simplify website building for small business owners and creative professionals. Before going to market, the marketing team had identified four potential product names and needed to know which one would resonate best with the target audience.

My job was to build and run a research process that produced a defensible recommendation — not just a preference ranking, but one grounded in user criteria the platform's target customers actually cared about.

## What I did

The project ran in **four stages**, each building on the last.

**Stage 1 — Knowledge Review.** I established the criteria that would define a strong product name for this context: memorability, ease of pronunciation, global appeal, understandability, and positive semantic associations. These became the evaluation dimensions for every subsequent stage.

**Stage 2 — Proof of Concept Survey.** I ran an unmoderated survey using Maze with **60 WordPress users** — predominantly self-identified as novice or beginner, with 65% working in creative fields or as company owners, matching the target demographic. Participants were asked to describe WordPress in three words and to vote on their preferred terms from the marketing team's initial name list.

Results: "Design" received the most votes (12), "Creator" came second (10). Participants generally found WordPress easy to use but expressed mixed confidence with its terminology. This guided which naming directions to develop further.

**Stage 3 — Creative Workshop.** I facilitated a workshop with marketing team members and project managers. Participants generated new name ideas, improved on existing candidates, and voted on their preferences. This session produced a refined list of six names to take into final validation.

**Stage 4 — Validation Survey.** I ran a second survey with **50 participants** representative of the target audience. Each participant rated all six names across the five criteria established in Stage 1, producing composite scores for comparison.

## What we found

The six names ranked as follows, from highest to lowest composite score:

1. **Wonder Blocks — 5.6** · Highest ratings for preferred name, easiest to remember, easiest to pronounce, and most positive associations
2. **WonderKit — 5.0** · Strong across preferred name, memorability, and pronunciation
3. **WP Creator Kit — 4.3** · Highest for positive associations among all items
4. **WP Design Kit — 3.5** · Middle ranking on understandability and positive associations
5. **WP Smart Blocks — 3.5** · Middle ranking on memorability and positive associations
6. **WP Content Kit — 2.6** · Lowest across preferred name, memorability, pronunciation, and understandability

The research identified a clear winner: **Wonder Blocks** led across the categories that matter most for product adoption — it was the name users preferred, could remember without effort, and associated positively with the product's purpose. The "WP" prefix pattern, used by four of the six candidates, consistently underperformed relative to the more distinctive two-word constructions.

## What changed

The research delivered a ranked, scored, and documented recommendation to the marketing team — giving them a defensible rationale for the final naming decision grounded in target-user responses rather than internal preference.

The four-stage structure was deliberate: the Knowledge Review ensured the right criteria were applied from the start; the Proof of Concept Survey generated user language before asking users to evaluate; the Creative Workshop integrated internal stakeholders; and the Validation Survey provided quantitative evidence to resolve disagreements.

The final naming decision rested with the marketing team, who had the recommendation and full scoring data to inform it.
