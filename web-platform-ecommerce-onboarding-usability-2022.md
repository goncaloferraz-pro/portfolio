# E-Commerce Store Setup Usability Testing — Web Services Platform

**Domain:** Web Hosting / SaaS
**Type:** Iterative Unmoderated Usability Testing
**Year:** 2022
**Role:** Senior UX Researcher

---

An enterprise web services platform was launching a new e-commerce package. Before release, the team needed to know whether new customers could successfully set up an online store after purchase — without guidance, in a realistic self-service scenario.

My job was to test the onboarding flow, identify friction, and iterate until the experience was ready.

## What I did

Over two weeks (July 2022), I planned and ran a **two-round iterative usability study** using Figma prototypes tested through an unmoderated remote testing platform.

The task scenario placed participants as someone who had just purchased an e-commerce hosting package and needed to set up a new online store. The **4 core tasks** covered the full onboarding flow:

1. Enter store address
2. Choose how to receive payments from customers
3. Add a new product
4. Make the store public and open for business

**Round 1 — Pilot** ran with **5 participants**: senior managers, directors, and business owners in the US and Canada, aged 35–65+, household income $100K–$150K+. This profile matched the platform's target e-commerce customer segment. After analyzing session recordings, I documented prototype issues, rated their severity, and worked with the design team to prioritize fixes.

**Seven prototype adjustments** were made before Round 2, including: viewport scaling, shipping info consistency, scroll position on the Tax Info page, site status flow behavior, and contextual content framing on plan pages.

**Round 2** ran as a full unmoderated study to validate the revised prototype and surface any residual issues.

## What we found

The results were strong across the board. **All participants completed all tasks**, and the average time to completion was **3 minutes** — indicating the flow was lean and well-sequenced.

Two findings stood out:

**Checkmarks on completed tasks were consistently praised.** Participants responded positively to the progress-tracking pattern — the visual confirmation of completed steps reduced uncertainty about where they were in the process and what remained. This was an unprompted observation across multiple participants.

**The Publish button was the primary friction point.** Participants struggled to locate it at the final step — the moment of making their store live. The issue was compounded by the absence of a website preview before going live: participants expected to see their store before making it public, and the flow did not provide that option. Both findings pointed to the same underlying gap: users needed clearer signaling at the moment of commitment.

A secondary finding emerged from qualitative feedback: participants wanted the system to distinguish between **required tasks** and **optional improvements** — a "things you have to do" vs. "things you might want to do" separation. This suggests a future enhancement to the onboarding checklist model.

## What changed

The two-round structure ensured that critical prototype issues were resolved before the study produced final results. The seven fixes made between rounds addressed the most significant interaction problems, allowing Round 2 to validate the flow rather than re-discover known issues.

The research gave the product team clear, evidence-based direction on two specific improvements needed before launch — Publish button visibility and pre-launch preview — while also confirming that the overall onboarding structure was sound: short, well-paced, and easy to complete once the final step was resolved.
